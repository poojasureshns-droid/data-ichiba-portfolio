Discover

Unifying thousands of datasets into one searchable platform.

The team’s current solution was finding data on SharePoint and Amazon Redshift. SharePoint made files hard to find and impossible to evaluate before opening. Redshift was powerful but locked out anyone without technical skills. Neither worked for most people.

Research

Understanding how people actually look for data.

I spoke with stakeholders from the data governance and analytics teams. Rather than jumping to solutions, I wanted to understand the mental model — how do employees think when they’re searching for a dataset?

  • What’s blocking people — is it access, discovery, or trust?
  • How do people actually search for data in practice?
  • What information do they need before requesting access?

The Problem

The data existed. Finding it was the problem.

01
02
03

No clear path from “I need data” to “I have data.”

Teams had no structured way to discover, evaluate, and get access to the data they needed. The process involved chasing the right person, waiting days for a response, and still not being sure the file was correct.

Finding a file meant clicking through folders blindly.

There was no shared knowledge base, no documentation, no way to understand what a dataset contained before opening it. Users couldn’t tell if what they found was relevant, reliable, or even the right file.

The search experience didn’t match how people think.

Existing tools required users to know exact file names. Nobody thinks that way. The search model was built for systems, not people.

Solution Ideation

Three things the platform needed to do well.

01
Discoverability
Search that works the way people think. Plain language, filters, trending datasets — find what you need without knowing the technical name.
02
Context
Each dataset surfaces what users actually need — owner, quality score, last refreshed, column glossary — so they can decide before ever requesting access.
03
Access
Instead of email and ServiceNow, request access directly in-app — with a defined approval chain, priority tiers, and real-time status updates.

The data governance team pushed back on this. Their concern was real — owning the request workflow inside the platform meant taking on more process, more roles, and more documentation burden. They were used to ServiceNow and email. That's where access requests lived, and they weren't eager to change that.

My goal was two-fold: reduce the steps from “I need this dataset” to “I have access”, and eliminate the context switching that was killing the experience. Every time someone left the app to file a ticket or send an email, they lost momentum — and often just gave up.

What moved the conversation

I mapped both journeys side by side — 11 steps outside the app vs 5 steps inside. The step count difference was stark. I didn’t win the argument with principles. I won it with the data.

This design prioritises
User autonomy · Zero context switching · Structured approval with real-time visibility · Accountability through a defined chain
The tradeoff
The governance team had to own a new workflow inside the product — a real operational ask they hadn’t planned for. I had to make that case repeatedly, with evidence, not just UX principles.
Design decision 02

Splitting search into two distinct modes

Early iterations of the search panel included domain filters, type filters, and keyword search all visible at once. My instinct was that more options meant more power. But testing told me something different.

Technical users found it manageable. Everyone else found it overwhelming — too many choices before they’d even typed anything. The interface was signalling “this is complicated” before users had a chance to discover it wasn’t.

The insight

Users weren’t all arriving with the same intent. Some were browsing. Some knew exactly what they wanted. The fix wasn’t removing features — it was letting users self-select the mode that matched their confidence.

Exploring
Domain-first browse. Pick a category, then scan. No text input required.
I know what I want
Type-ahead search with instant card results.

Design decision 03

The anatomy of a dataset card

Users might scan through dozens of cards looking for the right dataset. Every element on that card had to earn its place. Too much and it becomes noise. Too little and they can’t make a decision.

I landed on a progressive disclosure model. The card surface shows just enough to evaluate relevance at a glance — human-readable name, technical slug, domain, owner, certification badge, data quality rating, and last refresh date. Clicking opens a full detail view.

Key Learnings

What I’d take forward.

  • Stakeholder alignment is design work too.

    Getting the data governance and engineering teams to a shared vision was as much a design challenge as building the product itself.

  • Data beats principles.

    Telling stakeholders something is “better for users” rarely landed. Showing them the data always did.

  • Know when to push back — and when not to.

    Working across multiple teams means the brief shifts constantly. There were moments I had to hold the line, and moments where pushing back wasn’t the right move. Knowing the difference mattered.

  • AI for rapid iteration was a genuine win.

    It kept the design cycle moving when stakeholder reviews were frequent and feedback was fast.

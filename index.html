Overview

Bridgestone operates across 6 business functions — LSCM, Finance, Sales, Marketing, Commodity, and Operations. Every team relied on data to make decisions. But the data lived in two places that worked for almost no one.

Amazon Redshift
Built for engineers. Required knowledge of exact schema names like view__gold__str. Anyone without SQL fluency was locked out entirely.
SharePoint
Accessible to everyone, but organised in folders with no metadata, no search, and no way to know what a file contained before opening it.
The result
Teams chased data owners over email, waited days for access, and still weren’t sure they had the right file when it arrived.
Problem

A data tool built for developers,
used by everyone.

Bridgestone’s analysts, managers, and non-technical teams were expected to navigate a system that spoke in view__gold__sourcing and view__gold__str. If you didn’t know the schema, you couldn’t find anything.

Research

Understanding how people actually look for data.

I conducted interviews with 12 participants across two groups — data consumers and data owners — to understand the full picture of the problem from both sides.

8 Data consumers
Analysts, managers and non-technical users across LSCM, Finance, and Sales — varied data literacy, shared frustration. Focus: where the discovery process broke down and what information they needed before requesting access to a dataset.
4 Data owners
Engineers and data stewards responsible for maintaining datasets in Redshift. Focus: the volume of ad-hoc requests they fielded weekly, and what a structured pipeline would need to look like for them to actually trust it.
Research goals
01
What is blocking people — is it finding data, accessing it, or trusting it once they have it?
02
How do people actually search for data in practice — and how does that differ from how datasets are named?
03
What information do users need before they feel confident enough to request access?
What we found

No clear path from “I need data” to “I have data.”

Teams had no structured way to discover, evaluate, and get access to the data they needed. The process involved chasing the right person, waiting days for a response, and still not being sure the file was correct when it arrived.

A tool built for developers, used by everyone.

Existing tools required users to know exact file names like view__gold__str — a naming convention built for engineers, not analysts. Nobody searched that way. The search model was built for the system, not the people using it.

No way to evaluate data before requesting access.

There was no metadata, no data quality score, no column definitions visible before requesting access. Users had to commit to a request just to find out if the dataset was even relevant to their work.

North Star

Get the right information in front of users so they can independently decide whether what they’re looking at is the right dataset for them — before they ever request access.

Design decision 01

One search bar wasn’t enough.

Early iterations gave everyone the same search experience — a single bar, a single set of filters, visible all at once. Testing revealed two completely different users arriving with completely different intent.

Technical users found it manageable. Everyone else found it overwhelming — too many choices before they’d even typed anything.

The insight

Users weren’t arriving with the same intent. Some were browsing without a clear goal. Others knew exactly what they wanted. Designing one experience for both meant serving neither well.

Domain-first browse. Pick a business function or team, then scan what’s available by asset type. No search query required — designed for users who don’t know exactly what they’re looking for.

Type-ahead search with instant card results. Filter by domain, asset type (dataset, report, KPI), or certification status. For users who already know what they’re looking for and need to find it fast.

Design decision 02

How much do you show before someone clicks?

Everything a user needs to decide if a dataset is worth requesting — without having to request it first. I split this into two separate design problems.

Point 01 — The card and what it reveals

The card surface shows just enough to evaluate relevance at a glance: human-readable name, technical slug, domain, owner, certification badge, and last refresh date. Clicking opens a full detail view with four tabs — Overview, Sample set, Glossaries, and Comments — so users can go as deep as they need before deciding.

Point 02 — Trust and confidence signals

The information that actually builds trust isn’t the description. It’s the data quality scores (accuracy, completeness, correctness, credibility, consistency), comments from teammates who’ve used it, the rating, who’s downloaded it, and when it was last refreshed. These live in the Overview tab — impossible to miss.

Pushback

Should the request pipeline live inside the app or outside it?

I had to make the case to the data governance team to own a new workflow inside the product — a real operational ask they hadn’t planned for. The existing process was 7 steps across email, Teams, and ServiceNow. No visibility into who was reviewing the request. Users submitted and waited, with no feedback until access was granted or denied.

I showed them a side-by-side of the current 7-step process versus the in-app 3-step flow. The difference in time and steps was the argument.

The tradeoff

The governance team was worried about accountability — owning the pipeline meant owning a new operational role. Evidence, not UX principles, moved that conversation.

Key Learnings

What I’d take forward.

Stakeholder alignment is design work too

Getting the data governance and engineering teams to a shared vision was as much a design challenge as building the product itself. Neither group had the same definition of “done.”

Data beats principles

Telling stakeholders something is “better for users” rarely landed. Showing them the 7-step vs 3-step comparison always did. Evidence moved the conversation every time.

Know when to push back — and when not to

Working across multiple competing teams means the brief shifts constantly. There were moments I had to hold the line on the design. There were also moments where letting go was the right call — knowing the difference mattered more than being right.

Plain language beats technical precision

Nobody searched for view__gold__sourcing. Building a human-readable naming layer on top of technical nomenclature was a small decision with an outsized impact on discoverability across the platform.
